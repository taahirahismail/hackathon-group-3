What is credit?

Credit refers to the ability to borrow money or access goods or services with the promise of paying for them in the future. It is a financial arrangement between a borrower and a lender, where the borrower is provided with funds or a line of credit based on the expectation that they will repay the borrowed amount, often with interest, over a specified period of time.


Why do I need a credit score?

Having a credit score is important for several reasons:
  • Lender Evaluation

    • When you apply for a loan or credit, such as a mortgage, auto loan, or credit card, lenders use your credit score as one of the key factors to evaluate your creditworthiness.

  • Access to Credit

    • A good credit score makes it easier for you to access credit when you need it. Whether you're looking to finance a major purchase, start a business, or handle unexpected expenses, having a solid credit score opens up more borrowing options and allows you to take advantage of credit opportunities.

  • Rental Applications

    • andlords and property management companies often use credit scores to assess prospective tenants. A positive credit history demonstrates financial responsibility, making you a more desirable candidate for rental properties.

  • Utility Services and Cell Phone Contracts

    • Some utility companies and cell phone service providers may check your credit score before offering their services. A good credit score can help you qualify for utility services without requiring additional deposits or credit checks.

  • Employment Opportunities

    • In certain industries or job positions, employers may request permission to check your credit report as part of the hiring process. While the credit report doesn't typically include your credit score, a positive credit history can reflect positively on your financial responsibility and integrity.

  • Insurance Premiums

    • In some cases, insurance companies may consider your credit score when determining your insurance premiums. They view a higher credit score as an indicator of responsible behavior, potentially leading to lower premiums on auto, home, or renters' insurance.

  • Financial Planning

    • Your credit score provides a snapshot of your overall creditworthiness and financial health. Monitoring your credit score allows you to track your progress, identify areas for improvement, and take steps to build and maintain a strong credit history over time.

    How do I build a credit score?

  • Open a Bank Account

    • Begin by opening a checking or savings account in your name. While a bank account does not directly impact your credit score, it establishes a relationship with a financial institution and demonstrates financial stability.

  • Apply for a Secured Credit Card

    • A secured credit card is a good option for individuals with limited or no credit history. With a secured card, you provide a security deposit that becomes your credit limit. Use the card responsibly by making small purchases and paying the balance in full and on time each month.

  • Become an Authorized User

    • If you have a family member or close friend with a good credit history, you may ask them to add you as an authorized user on their credit card. As an authorized user, the card's positive payment history and responsible credit usage will be reflected on your credit report, helping you build credit.

  • Apply for a Credit-Builder Loan

    • Some financial institutions offer credit-builder loans specifically designed to help individuals build credit. With this type of loan, the funds are typically held in an account while you make monthly payments. Once the loan is repaid, you receive the funds, and the positive payment history is reported to credit bureaus, boosting your credit score.

  • Make Timely Payments

    • Payment history is a crucial factor in determining your credit score. Make sure to pay all bills, such as credit card bills, loans, and utility bills, on time. Late payments can have a negative impact on your credit score, so it's essential to establish a track record of timely payments.

    Is credit bad?

  • Debt Accumulation

    • One of the risks associated with credit is the potential to accumulate excessive debt. When individuals borrow more than they can afford to repay or consistently carry high balances on credit cards without a plan to pay them off, it can lead to financial strain and difficulties in meeting repayment obligations.

  • Interest Charges

    • Credit often comes with interest charges. When you carry a balance on a credit card or take out loans, you are typically charged interest on the outstanding balance. If you only make minimum payments or fail to pay off the balance in full, the interest charges can accumulate, leading to additional financial burdens.

  • Negative Impact on Credit Score

    • Irresponsible credit management, such as missing payments, defaulting on loans, or having high credit card balances, can negatively impact your credit score. A lower credit score can make it harder to access credit in the future or result in higher interest rates when you do borrow.

  • Impulse Spending

    • Credit cards can make it easy to spend money without immediate financial consequences. This convenience can sometimes lead to impulse buying or overspending beyond one's means, which can create financial stress down the line.
